The Cost to Buy Wins in the NBA

A Data-Driven Analysis of Offensive vs. Defensive Wins

There is a clear correlation between how good players are and how much they get paid. But as it turns out, salaries reflect mostly only offensive value.

By using a multiple linear regression model on player salary and their Offensive and Defensive Wins Above Replacement (WAR), we can determine the marginal "market price" for an extra win produced in each category, based on players' individual contributions on both ends of the floor and how much they're actually getting paid.

The best players in the NBA tend to be offensive stars and deserve their high salaries. But are we appropriately valuing the league as a whole?

The Market Price of a Single Win Produced

Over the last five seasons, a win produced was worth $2.45M. But not all wins have been compensated equally.

Offensive wins have cost roughly 4 times as much money to buy as defensive wins.

The average salary increase for each additional offensive win produced. This is where teams overspend.

DEFENSE: THE VALUE MARKET

The average salary increase for each additional defensive win produced. This represents a significant opportunity for efficient roster building.

Teams Underrate Defense for Guards and Wings

Bigs tend to rate slightly better defensively, and they tend to be underpaid. But this does NOT explain the offense/defense discrepancy.

In fact, when looking at just guards and wings, they are paid 15 times as much money for their offensive contributions as their defense.

$2,023,000 Guard/Wing Offensive Win
$133,500 Guard/Wing Defensive Win

OFFENSE: THE PREMIUM MARKET

Nearly all of guard/wing salaries are determined by their offensive performance.

DEFENSE: THE VALUE MARKET

There is very little difference in the salaries between good and bad defensive players, despite wins being just as impactful for guards and wings.

How Players Have Been Paid

This chart shows how players have been paid over the last five seasons, based on either their offensive or defensive percentile (rookie scale contracts excluded). As shown in the chart, as players get better offensively, their salary rises accordingly. But on the defensive end, there is a very small gap in salary between the best defensive players and the worst ones.


The top 250 defenders over the past five seasons have made an average of $18M per season. The top 250 offensive players have made an average of $29M.

Great Teams Collect Great Defenders

Projected top 10 teams this year have about 0.4 more very good offensive players (top 25th percentile) than middle 10 teams. But they have 2.0 more very good defenders.
